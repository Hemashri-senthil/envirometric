What is AQI?

The Air Quality Index (AQI) helps indicate how clean or polluted the air is, and what associated health effects might be a concern. It ranges from 0 (Good) to 500 (Hazardous). AQI simplifies multiple air pollution measurements into a single number and category. It enables the public to quickly understand current air quality and take precautions if necessary. AQI considers pollutants like PM2.5, PM10, CO, NO₂, SO₂, and O₃. Monitoring AQI helps reduce health risks and plan safer outdoor activities.

Sources of Pollution

Sources of air pollution include a wide range of human and natural activities. Major contributors are vehicle emissions, industrial factories, coal-fired power plants, construction activities, and the burning of fossil fuels. Agricultural practices like crop burning and the use of pesticides also release harmful pollutants. Wildfires and dust storms are natural sources that significantly degrade air quality, especially in dry regions. These sources emit dangerous pollutants such as PM2.5, nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), sulfur dioxide (SO₂), and volatile organic compounds (VOCs).

Common Pollutants

  • PM2.5 – Fine particulate matter
  • PM10 – Dust and pollen
  • Ozone (O₃)
  • Carbon Monoxide (CO)
  • Nitrogen Dioxide (NO₂)
  • Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂)
